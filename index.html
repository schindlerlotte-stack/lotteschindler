Stem Cell Research · Stanford University
Primitive vs. Definitive Hematopoiesis: Lineage Tracing in Embryonic Vascular Development
Weissman Labs · Stanford, California · 2026

The earliest blood in mammals arises in the yolk sac blood islands, where mesodermal precursors generate primitive erythroid cells and macrophage-lineage progenitors before the emergence of true long-term hematopoietic stem cells (LT-HSCs). This project investigates how those two programs, primitive and definitive, are molecularly and anatomically distinguished, and what role genetic maternal factors play in shaping early vascular identity.

Research Detail & Methods

Scientific Context

The modern consensus places the origin of definitive LT-HSCs at the hemogenic endothelium of the aorta-gonad-mesonephros (AGM) region, not the yolk sac itself. The key event is the endothelial-to-hematopoietic transition (EHT), in which arterial endothelial cells bud into the aortic lumen and form intra-aortic hematopoietic clusters. Runx1 is essential for this transition. Yolk sac erythro-myeloid progenitors (EMPs), by contrast, are now understood as the principal embryonic source of tissue-resident macrophages and microglia, which seed fetal tissues early and self-maintain with minimal adult bone marrow input. A central open question is how much of steady-state adult hematopoiesis is genuinely supplied by classical LT-HSCs versus long-lived downstream multipotent progenitors.

Our Approach

Working under award-winning scientist Dr. Irving Weissman, we use inducible Cre-lox fate mapping with temporally controlled drivers to label discrete progenitor populations and track their progeny across embryonic and adult timepoints. Driver choice is critical: constitutive endothelial drivers such as Cdh5/VE-cadherin capture broad vascular histories, while inducible systems allow tighter developmental gating. Experimental variables related to genetic maternal influence on early blood island formation are extracted and structured to test whether maternal genotype shapes the primitive hematopoietic niche before EHT-derived HSCs emerge.

Global Health Initiative · Belize
Belize Maternal Health Initiative
Tulane Global Health Alliance · Arenal, Belize · 2025–Present · Led by Dr. Lorelei Cropley

A community-based participatory research initiative addressing the social and environmental determinants of maternal health outcomes in Arenal, Belize, in partnership with a local midwife and under the guidance of Dr. Lorelei Cropley. The project integrates epidemiological fieldwork with culturally grounded care frameworks adapted to the Mayan community context.

Initiative Details

Public Health Framework

The initiative applies a social determinants of health lens to understand the structural and environmental risk factors driving adverse maternal and neonatal outcomes in a rural, resource-limited setting. Environmental health factors including water access, sanitation infrastructure, and geographic isolation are assessed as upstream determinants of maternal morbidity. Epidemiological methods inform needs assessment and resource allocation, with attention to incidence patterns of obstetric complications and barriers to skilled birth attendance in the catchment population.

Ethics & Cultural Approach

Working within a Mayan community requires active engagement with indigenous research ethics, including principles of community consent, reciprocity, and the co-production of knowledge with local knowledge holders. Traditional Mayan birth practices and the role of the local midwife as a trusted cultural authority are treated as assets rather than barriers. The initiative is designed to strengthen, not supplant, existing community care infrastructure, and all resource provision is negotiated collaboratively to avoid imposing externally defined clinical standards on a community with its own epistemology of health.

A Little About Me

I grew up vacillating between Austrian and American culture, a dual existence that shaped how I see the world and drew me toward medical anthropology and the ways geography quietly governs human health. I am fascinated by the question of why place determines fate, and by how culture, landscape, and history write themselves onto the body.

Outside of work and study, I decompress on a tennis court or in the ocean. Skiing in the Alps still feels like home. I journal, meditate, and read whenever I can. My all-time favourite remains The Goldfinch by Donna Tartt.
