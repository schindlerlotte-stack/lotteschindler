Stem Cell Research · Stanford University · Weissman Labs
Yolk Sac Hematopoiesis, Microglial Lineage, and Embryonic Fate Mapping
Under Dr. Irving Weissman · Stanford, California · 2026

Working under award-winning scientist Dr. Irving Weissman, this research investigates the developmental origins of blood and brain-resident immune cells using inducible Cre-lox lineage tracing in mouse embryos. The central biological question is how the earliest blood programs in the yolk sac, specifically primitive erythropoiesis and erythro-myeloid progenitor (EMP) waves, are spatiotemporally separated from the definitive hematopoietic stem cell (HSC) program that arises later from hemogenic endothelium in the aorta-gonad-mesonephros (AGM). The modern consensus places the first bona fide long-term HSCs at the AGM, where arterial endothelial cells undergo endothelial-to-hematopoietic transition (EHT), a Runx1-dependent budding event that generates intra-aortic hematopoietic clusters (IAHCs). Yolk sac EMPs, by contrast, are the principal embryonic source of tissue-resident macrophages and microglia, which seed fetal tissues early and self-maintain with minimal adult bone marrow input. A central open question is whether there are distinct microglial subpopulations with different embryonic origins, renewal capacities, or transcriptional identities. These three projects address that question at complementary developmental windows using ultrasound-guided injection, immunohistochemistry, and single-cell transcriptomics.

Project 01 Ultrasound-Guided Microinjection into the Yolk Sac at E8.25–8.5

Experimental Design

Ultrasound-guided microinjections deliver a micro-dose of 4-hydroxytamoxifen (4-OHT) directly into the space between the amnion and the mesodermal surface of the visceral yolk sac in Hoxb5-CreERT2; R26-zsGreen embryos at E8.25–8.5. This timing targets the primitive and early EMP hematopoietic waves before the emergence of AGM-derived definitive HSCs. The approach could also be adapted to deliver viruses, chemical inhibitors or activators, or exogenous cells depending on experimental needs. Embryos are harvested at E9.5 and E10.5–E11.5, with tissue collected from the dorsal aorta (to examine IAHCs) and the developing brain (to examine early microglial seeding).

Open Questions

Can we confine 4-OHT delivery to the yolk sac compartment through this injection strategy, thereby restricting CreERT2 activation to yolk sac progenitors and avoiding recombination in intra-embryonic hemogenic endothelium?

Can direct intra-yolk sac injection of 4-OHT induce recombination in all Hoxb5+ cells within that compartment, and is recombination efficiency sufficient to detect labeled progeny at later harvest points?

Project 02 Immunostaining of E14.5 Hoxb5 Lineage-Traced Brains

Experimental Design

Using existing E14.5 lineage-traced brain samples (in collaboration with Tiffany), tissue is embedded, cryosectioned, and stained against a panel of markers: Iba1 and CD45 to identify microglia and myeloid identity, and Ki67/Mcm2 to assess proliferative activity. An additional arm of the project involves staining against neurotransmitter markers to test whether zsGreen+ (yolk sac-derived) microglia are preferentially positioned near specific neuronal cell types, probing potential functional heterogeneity in early microglial colonization.

Open Questions

How soon after yolk sac specification do EMP-derived microglia enter the neural tube and brain parenchyma, and is their entry kinetics distinct from later monocyte-derived populations?

What proportion of zsGreen+ (yolk sac-lineage) microglia are actively dividing at E14.5, and does this differ from zsGreen− microglia?

Are yolk sac-derived microglia preferentially localized to distinct brain regions or near specific neuronal populations, suggesting early functional patterning?

Project 03 De Novo Lineage Tracing, FACS Sorting, and scRNA-seq of zsGreen+ Microglia

Experimental Design

This arm sets up a full lineage-tracing pipeline from breeding through single-cell transcriptomics. Hoxb5-CreERT2; R26-zsGreen crosses are mated, plug dates recorded, and 4-OHT administered at defined developmental timepoints to induce recombination. After harvest, zsGreen+ microglia are isolated by fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) and submitted for single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) processing. Unbiased clustering and gene expression profiling will characterize transcriptional heterogeneity within the yolk sac-derived microglial population and identify candidate subsets that resemble less-differentiated hematopoietic progenitors or quiescent microglial precursors. Results are expected post-summer.

Open Questions

Are there transcriptionally distinct subsets within the zsGreen+ microglial population, and do any retain a gene expression signature consistent with a less-differentiated or progenitor-like hematopoietic state?

Does unbiased scRNA-seq clustering reveal microglial heterogeneity that correlates with anatomical location, proliferative state, or proximity to specific neuronal cell types observed in Project 02?

Global Health Initiative · Belize
Belize Maternal Health Initiative
Tulane Global Health Alliance · Arenal, Belize · 2025–Present · Led by Dr. Lorelei Cropley

Founded and led entirely from scratch, the Tulane Global Health Alliance (TUGHA) was built as a student organization with no prior infrastructure, and this initiative is its flagship project. Under the guidance of Dr. Lorelei Cropley, the Belize Maternal Health Initiative is a community-based participatory research project addressing the social and environmental determinants of maternal health outcomes in Arenal, Belize, in partnership with a local midwife. The project integrates epidemiological fieldwork and quantitative analysis with culturally grounded care frameworks adapted to the Mayan community context.

Initiative Details

Epidemiological Framework

The initiative applies a social determinants of health lens to identify structural and environmental risk factors driving adverse maternal and neonatal outcomes in a rural, resource-limited setting. Epidemiological methods inform needs assessment and resource prioritization, with attention to incidence and prevalence patterns of obstetric complications, rates of skilled birth attendance, and geographic barriers to care access in the catchment population. Environmental health factors including water quality, sanitation infrastructure, and household-level exposures are assessed as upstream determinants of maternal morbidity. Descriptive and analytic epidemiology methods are applied to field-collected data, with statistical analysis conducted in SPSS and Qualtrics used for survey data collection and management.

Ethics & Cultural Approach

Working within a Mayan community requires active engagement with indigenous research ethics, including principles of community consent, reciprocity, and the co-production of knowledge with local knowledge holders. Traditional Mayan birth practices and the role of the local midwife as a trusted cultural authority are treated as assets rather than barriers. The initiative is designed to strengthen, not supplant, existing community care infrastructure, and all resource provision is negotiated collaboratively to avoid imposing externally defined clinical standards on a community with its own epistemology of health.
